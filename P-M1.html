Peer Review Comments

It timely applies regular sentiment analysis to the student feedback collected and uses the Naive Bayes and SVM models in particular. A logical approach to solving the problems has been used and the time table adopted is perfectly reasonable. However, unstructured data may pose a challenge to actionable insights, due to the fact that they are brought into the model without prior structure. The increase of attention to data preprocessing may improve the quality of analysis. The research focuses on an important question in the field of education, which outlines the approach to sentiment analysis specifically and clearly. The application of multiple models shows that it is systematic. This research could have benefitted from a more detailed conversation on the data preprocessing strategies to enhance the management of unstructured feedback.

For SYNNER group, using sentiment analysis to evaluate student feedback after school events is clear and well-structured. It effectively outlines the problem of handling unstructured feedback data and the need to transform it into useful insights for school improvement. Using models like BERT, Naive Bayes, and SVM is a practical choice, as each provides unique advantages for analyzing sentiment. Overall, this project shows a good approach to applying sentiment analysis in education and could genuinely help schools understand and act on student feedback.

Date: Nov 1 at 5:01pm

It is noticeable that there are weaknesses that the SYNNER Project Proposal is showing based on their IEEE report, which complicates its feasibility and comprehension: One significant flaw is no Gantt chart, or semblance of one in the form of a pipeline which hinders in tracking back the project timeline and realization of how tasks will be achieved and when. This paper also lacks a comprehensive elaboration of the sentiment analysis models or model architecture which brings out issues on the planned method for analyzing the feedback. Unfortunately, there are no specific models mentioned and architectures make it very hard to determine the appropriateness of the chosen techniques on educational feedback data. Also, the proposal lacks important points of concern as regards text preprocessing. It has left out how the unstructured feedback will be standardized and the kind of control that will be put in place to deal with challenges such as slang, and spelling, and colloquial language. Additionally, the proposal does not envision multiple-language feedback, which would be highly problematic since analysis would be accompanied by language detection and translation preprocessing steps. These oversights might mean that the problem is not sufficiently deep to deal with the issues of realistic sentiment analysis, and more detailed preparation of approaches to these problems is necessary. All in all, it gives a rather vague and incomplete impression of the proposal and its links to the actual ways of applying NLP to analyze educational feedback.
